Extra mile for a better mobile app UX

Extending a cross-device feature

As a UX designer, I was working in 2 feature teams of our client, a German car company, improving their mobile app. One team owned the feature to update the car's firmware via phone, and the latest generation cars enabled the starting and following installations by phone – functionality that should be designed and implemented in the companion app.

The firmware update flow should be extended with new functionality.

Creating a clear framing first

The start was confusing, as the product owner (PO) already created a UX concept to speed up the implementation, which also included some improvements. Not being familiar with the feature yet, I first sorted the information in:

  • Business and customer goals
  • Technical requirements for new functionality
  • Ideas for new functionality
  • Improvements for existing part

Part of the UX concept prepared by my product owner

Fostering a productive relationship

I perceived that the PO's emphasis on speed prevented us to create the necessary UX quality, so I assessed his rationals for focusing on speed this much:

  1. Upcoming "Christmas Testing" where new functionality is showcased to senior management
  2. Delays caused by other designers in the past, who struggled with the team's highly-technical topics
  3. Loads of other features waited to be built

The following activities reassured the PO of our progress, and unlocked a collaborative style of work:

  • From the start, make progress visible to increase PO's trust: Requirements & designs accessible on Confluence, weekly sharing sessions
  • Collaborate closely with PO & tech lead to understand technical limitations and possibilities
  • Align on business and customer goals, and guide design discussions with them

Business context & usability challenges

There are 3 ways for customers to update their car's firmware, with different cost implications for the manufacturer.

I – Download to phone & transfer to car

Albeit not being straightforward, this is the standard method for the manufacturer, as it uses the phone's Wi-Fi connection and does not come with extra costs.

II – Direct download to car

I expected this to be the standard method, and product analytics showed many users did so as well. While more convenient for users, it comes with an average of 5€ cellular costs per update for the manufacturer – amounting to more than 1 Million € due to the fleet's scale.

III – Installation in car workshop

As a fallback, firmware updates can be performed during workshop visits – causing 100€ extra cost per installation for the manufacturer.

Evangelizing for usability

Copying the car's update flow, as in the PO's concept, to reduce implementation efforts entails UX problems: When starting an update in the car, installation blockers like a low battery or an open door are shown next so users can fix the issue.

Installation states in the car

PO's concept based on technical state model

Blockers are not shown before starting an installation, which – in the mobile app – creates a disappointing experience when users see the "Start update" button and only learn after clicking that one of 16 issues is blocking the installation.

It was necessary to work closely together with the PO and tech lead to assess what is technically possible, and to convince the PO to invest into adjusting the technical flow albeit the time pressure, to achieve this small change in the user flow:
